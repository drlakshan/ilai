# EPISTAXIS (Nose Bleeding) Simplified

---

## Overview and Classification

<details>

<summary><strong>Definition</strong></summary>

<ul>

<li>Bleeding from the nose; a common ENT emergency.</li>

</ul>

</details>

<details>

<summary><strong>Classification by Location</strong></summary>

<ul>

<li><strong>Anterior:</strong> Easily located and managed by simple means.</li>

<li><strong>Posterior:</strong> Challenging to manage; often requires specialized treatment.</li>

</ul>

</details>

<details>

<summary><strong>Classification by Etiology</strong></summary>

<ul>

<li><strong>Primary/Idiopathic:</strong> Accounts for 70-80% of cases.</li>

<li><strong>Secondary:</strong> Occurs due to trauma, surgery, or medication.</li>

</ul>

</details>

---

## Vascular Anatomy and Key Bleeding Sites

<details>

<summary><strong>Arterial Supply Sources</strong></summary>

<ul>

<li><strong>Internal Carotid Artery (ICA):</strong> Supplies via its ethmoidal branches.</li>

<li><strong>External Carotid Artery (ECA):</strong> Supplies via its facial and maxillary branches, with the Sphenopalatine Artery being a key vessel.</li>

</ul>

</details>

<details>

<summary><strong>Key Bleeding Plexus</strong></summary>

<ul>

<li><strong>Little's Area / Kiesselbach's Plexus:</strong> Located on the anterior septum and is a common site for nosebleeds.</li>

<li><strong>Woodruff's Plexus:</strong> Found in the posterior/inferior turbinate region.</li>

</ul>

</details>

<details>

<summary><strong>Surgical Landmarks</strong></summary>

<ul>

<li><strong>Anterior Ethmoidal Artery (AEA):</strong> At risk during Endoscopic Sinus Surgery (ESS).</li>

<li><strong>Sphenopalatine Foramen (SPF):</strong> The site for major artery ligation, known as Endoscopic Sphenopalatine Artery Ligation (ESPAL).</li>

</ul>

</details>

---

## Management Strategies (Adult Primary Epistaxis)

<details>

<summary><strong>Initial/First Line Treatment</strong></summary>

<ul>

<li><strong>First Aid:</strong> Pinching the soft part of the nose (ala nasi) and resuscitation if necessary.</li>

<li><strong>Direct Therapy:</strong> Cautery (using silver nitrate or bipolar diathermy) for visible bleeding sources.</li>

</ul>

</details>

<details>

<summary><strong>Intermediate/Indirect Therapy</strong></summary>

<ul>

<li><strong>Nasal Packing:</strong> Using anterior or posterior packs for temporary control.</li>

<li><strong>Endoscopic Assessment:</strong> To locate the source if packing fails.</li>

<li><strong>Adjuvant Medical Therapy:</strong> Includes medications like Tranexamic acid.</li>

</ul>

</details>

<details>

<summary><strong>Surgical/Definitive Control (For Failed Conservative Treatment)</strong></summary>

<ul>

<li><strong>Artery Ligation:</strong> Endoscopic Sphenopalatine Artery Ligation (ESPAL) is the preferred procedure.</li>

<li><strong>Angiographic Embolization:</strong> Reserved for complex cases or when surgery fails.</li>

</ul>

</details>

---

## Secondary Causes of Epistaxis

<details>

<summary><strong>Trauma</strong></summary>

<ul>

<li>Includes fractures and carries a risk of Internal Carotid Artery (ICA) pseudo-aneurysm.</li>

</ul>

</details>

<details>

<summary><strong>Medication Use</strong></summary>

<ul>

<li>Associated with anticoagulants like Warfarin or Novel Oral Anticoagulants (NOACs).</li>

</ul>

</details>

<details>

<summary><strong>Systemic Disease/Conditions</strong></summary>

<ul>

<li>Hereditary Hemorrhagic Telangiectasia (HHT).</li>

<li>Tumors, such as Juvenile Nasopharyngeal Angiofibroma.</li>

</ul>

</details>
