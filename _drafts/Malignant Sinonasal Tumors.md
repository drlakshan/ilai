# Malignant Sinonasal Tumors

## Overview
Malignant tumors of the nasal cavity and paranasal sinuses are rare, accounting for <1% of all malignancies and ~3% of upper aerodigestive tract cancers. Late presentation is common due to non-specific symptoms mimicking chronic rhinosinusitis.

## Classification by Histological Type

### 1. Epithelial Malignancies

#### Squamous Cell Carcinoma (SCC)
**Most common** sinonasal malignancy (50-60%)
- **Location**: Maxillary sinus >> nasal cavity > ethmoid sinuses
- **Risk factors**:
  - Occupational exposure (wood dust, leather, nickel, chromium)
  - Human papillomavirus (HPV) - especially inverted papilloma-associated
  - Chronic rhinosinusitis
- **Subtypes**:
  - Keratinizing
  - Non-keratinizing
  - Basaloid
  - Papillary
- **Prognosis**: Variable, T-stage dependent

#### Adenocarcinoma
Second most common (10-20%)
- **Intestinal type (ITAC)**:
  - **Strong occupational link**: Wood dust exposure (furniture workers)
  - Ethmoid sinuses predominantly
  - Better prognosis than SCC
- **Non-intestinal type**:
  - Low-grade: Better outcomes
  - High-grade: Aggressive behavior

#### Sinonasal Undifferentiated Carcinoma (SNUC)
**Highly aggressive** neuroendocrine carcinoma
- Rapid growth, early metastasis
- Ethmoid sinuses > nasal cavity
- Poor prognosis despite multimodal therapy
- **DDx**: Must distinguish from [[Extranodal NK-T Cell Lymphoma]], olfactory neuroblastoma

#### Neuroendocrine Carcinomas
- **Small cell carcinoma**: Similar to pulmonary small cell
- **Large cell neuroendocrine carcinoma**
- Both highly aggressive, systemic therapy required

#### Adenoid Cystic Carcinoma
- **Salivary gland origin** (minor salivary glands)
- **Perineural invasion** characteristic
- Slow growth but relentless progression
- Late distant metastases (lungs, bone)
- Long-term survival poor despite initial response
- Related: [[malignant salivary tumours]]

#### Mucoepidermoid Carcinoma
- Minor salivary gland origin
- Low, intermediate, high grade variants
- Better prognosis than adenoid cystic

### 2. Neuroectodermal Tumors

#### [[Olfactory Neuroblastoma (Esthesioneuroblastoma)]]
- **Origin**: Olfactory epithelium (cribriform plate)
- **Bimodal age**: 2nd and 6th decades
- **Location**: Superior nasal cavity, cribriform region
- **Grading**: Hyams grading (I-IV)
- **Kadish staging**: A (nasal), B (paranasal), C (beyond), D (distant mets)
- **Treatment**: Surgery + radiation ± chemotherapy
- **Prognosis**: Grade and stage dependent
- **Complications**:
  - CSF leak risk
  - Intracranial extension
  - See: [[CSFLeaks]]

#### Sinonasal Melanoma
**Primary mucosal melanoma** - rare but highly aggressive
- 1% of all melanomas
- **Location**: Nasal cavity > maxillary sinus
- **Presentation**: Pigmented or amelanotic mass, epistaxis
- **Prognosis**: Very poor (5-yr survival 20-30%)
- **Treatment**: Wide surgical excision primary treatment
- Immunotherapy role evolving
- Related: [[MelanomaoftheHeadAndNeck]]

### 3. Lymphomas

#### Extranodal NK/T-Cell Lymphoma, Nasal Type
**Most common** sinonasal lymphoma
- **EBV-associated** (EBER positive)
- **CD56+** on immunohistochemistry
- **Angiocentric, angiodestructive** → ischemic necrosis
- **Clinical**: Relentlessly progressive midline destruction
- **Historic name**: "Lethal midline granuloma"
- **Critical DDx**: [[Granulomatosis with Polyangiitis (GPA)]], [[Cocaine-Induced Midline Destructive Lesions]]
- **Misdiagnosis catastrophic**: Immunosuppression accelerates tumor
- See: [[Extranodal NK-T Cell Lymphoma]], [[Midline Destructive Lesions of the Nose]]

#### Diffuse Large B-Cell Lymphoma (DLBCL)
- Second most common sinonasal lymphoma
- Better prognosis than NK/T-cell
- Responds to R-CHOP chemotherapy

#### Other Lymphomas
- Burkitt lymphoma
- Follicular lymphoma
- Mantle cell lymphoma
- [[B Symptoms]] may be present

### 4. Sarcomas

#### Rhabdomyosarcoma
**Most common** soft tissue sarcoma in children
- **Subtypes**:
  - Embryonal (most common in head/neck)
  - Alveolar (worse prognosis)
- **Peak age**: <10 years
- **Location**: Orbit, nasopharynx, paranasal sinuses
- **Treatment**: Multimodal (chemo + surgery/radiation)

#### Chondrosarcoma
- Skull base origin (sphenoid, ethmoid)
- Slow-growing
- Surgery mainstay (radiation-resistant)

#### Osteosarcoma
- Rare in sinonasal tract
- Maxilla > mandible in head/neck
- Aggressive, multimodal therapy

#### Fibrosarcoma
- Rare
- Adults > children
- Variable grade and prognosis

#### Angiosarcoma
- Highly aggressive vascular sarcoma
- Poor prognosis

### 5. Other Malignancies

#### Metastatic Disease
**Primary sites**:
- Renal cell carcinoma (characteristic hypervascular)
- Lung carcinoma
- Breast carcinoma
- Thyroid carcinoma
- Prostate carcinoma

#### Plasmacytoma
- Solitary or multiple myeloma manifestation
- Radiosensitive
- Monitor for progression to systemic myeloma

## Premalignant/Malignant Transformation Risk

### Inverted Papilloma
- **Malignant transformation**: 5-15%
- Usually to squamous cell carcinoma
- **HPV association** in some cases
- Complete surgical excision essential
- Long-term surveillance required

### Schneiderian Papillomas
- Inverted (most common, malignant potential)
- Fungiform (low malignant potential)
- Oncocytic (rare, low malignant potential)

## Clinical Presentation - Red Flags

### Symptoms Suggesting Malignancy
- **Unilateral nasal obstruction** (progressive)
- **Epistaxis** (especially recurrent, unilateral)
- **Facial pain/numbness** (infraorbital nerve V2)
- **Proptosis, diplopia** (orbital invasion)
- **Trismus** (pterygoid involvement)
- **Palatal bulge/ulceration**
- **Neck mass** (nodal metastasis)
- **B symptoms** in lymphomas

### Advanced Disease Signs
- **Cranial nerve palsies** (especially CN II, III, IV, V, VI)
- **CSF rhinorrhea** (skull base erosion)
- **Visual changes** (optic nerve compression)
- **Facial deformity**

## Diagnostic Workup

### Essential Investigations
1. **Nasal endoscopy** - [[Rigid Nasal Endoscopy]]
2. **Imaging**:
   - **CT sinuses**: Bone destruction patterns
   - **MRI head/neck**: Soft tissue extension, perineural spread, intracranial involvement
   - **PET-CT**: Staging, nodal/distant metastases
3. **Tissue diagnosis**:
   - **Adequate deep biopsy** essential
   - Immunohistochemistry
   - Special stains as indicated
4. **Staging investigations** per tumor type

## Treatment Principles

### Multimodal Approach
**Surgery**:
- Open approaches: Lateral rhinotomy, midfacial degloving, craniofacial resection
- Endoscopic approaches: Selected cases
- Skull base techniques for advanced disease
- Related: [[Ensocopic Sinus Surgery - Ethmoidectomy]], [[Pituitary surgery trans sphenoidal details]]

**Radiation therapy**:
- Adjuvant or definitive
- IMRT preferred (organ preservation)
- Proton therapy emerging

**Chemotherapy**:
- Neoadjuvant, concurrent, adjuvant
- Tumor-type specific protocols
- Primary for lymphomas

**Immunotherapy**:
- Evolving role in melanoma, selected carcinomas

## Prognosis

### Generally Poor Factors
- Late presentation (most present T3/T4)
- Anatomical complexity (limits complete resection)
- Proximity to critical structures
- High-grade histologies
- Perineural/intracranial extension
- Nodal metastases

### 5-Year Survival (Approximate)
- SCC: 30-60% (stage-dependent)
- Adenocarcinoma (ITAC): 40-60%
- SNUC: 20-40%
- Esthesioneuroblastoma: 45-65%
- Sinonasal melanoma: 20-30%
- NK/T-cell lymphoma: 30-50%
- DLBCL: 50-70%

## Differential Diagnosis

### Benign Mimickers

## Key Clinical Pearls

1. **Unilateral symptoms in adults** = malignancy until proven otherwise
2. **Biopsy adequacy critical**: Superficial biopsy may miss diagnosis
3. **Occupational history essential**: Wood dust, leather, industrial exposures
4. **HPV testing**: Consider in inverted papilloma-associated SCC
5. **EBV/EBER**: Mandatory for suspected NK/T-cell lymphoma
6. **Perineural invasion**: Adenoid cystic → long-term follow-up essential
7. **Avoid immunosuppression** until lymphoma excluded in midline destructive lesions
