# Sri Lankan Audiology Context & Healthcare Landscape
## Critical Data for Presentation Relevance

---

## HEARING LOSS BURDEN IN SRI LANKA

### Prevalence Statistics
- **~300,000 Sinhala speakers** are deaf or hard of hearing (DHH)
- **26.7% of disabled population** has hearing or speaking impairment
- **5.5% of school-age children** in Gampaha district have hearing impairment
- **8.8% moderate to severe** hearing impairment (2004 estimate - likely higher now)

### Demographic Reality
- Population of ~22 million
- If 8.8% prevalence: **~1.9 million people** with moderate-severe hearing loss
- School screening shows substantial childhood burden
- Aging population will increase prevalence further

### Public Health Significance
- Hearing/speech impairment is the **most common sensory disability** in Sri Lanka
- DHH community **marginalized and under-supported**
- Educational and social needs require urgent attention

---

## AUDIOLOGY WORKFORCE CRISIS

### The Numbers (Critical Gap)
- **0.18 audiologists/audiology technicians per 100,000 population**
  - For comparison: Need ~1-2 per 100,000 for adequate coverage
  - **Current: ~40 audiology technicians for 22 million people**
  - **Required: ~220-440 audiologists minimum**
  - **Gap: 180-400 professionals SHORT**

### Government Sector Reality
- Government hospitals **DO NOT have audiologists**
- Only **audiology technicians** (lower qualification level)
- **No recruiting of audiologists** to government sector
- Wide variation in specialist-to-population ratio between districts

### Training Pipeline
- **Single BSc programme** at University of Kelaniya (Speech & Hearing Sciences)
- Established 1991 - relatively recent profession in Sri Lanka
- Before late 1990s: **virtually NO SLTs or audiologists**
- Only one UK-visiting SLT for 6 months/year
- Training capacity limited - cannot meet demand

### Professional Cadre Status
- SLTs and audiologists **have recognized cadre** status
- BUT: Not being recruited to government positions
- Pushes professionals to private sector
- Creates access inequality

---

## INFRASTRUCTURE & SERVICE DISTRIBUTION

### Government Hospital Coverage
- **647 total government hospitals** (providing free healthcare)
- **Only 33 hospitals** (5%) have audiology units
- **614 hospitals** (95%) have NO audiology services

### Geographic Distribution (SEVERE INEQUALITY)
- **Private sector**: 77 speech therapy centres in 15 of 25 districts (60%)
- **Private audiology**: 36 centres in 9 of 25 districts (36%)
- **10 districts** completely without audiological services
- **Major concentration**: Colombo and Western Province
- **Rural areas**: Virtually no access

### Service Limitations
- **Government sector**: "Very basic and limited" audiology services
- Limited provision of hearing aids
- Limited cochlear implants (Lady Ridgeway Hospital has first state sector CI programme)
- Advanced equipment/services concentrated in private sector

### Cochlear Implant Programme
- **Lady Ridgeway Hospital**: First state sector CI programme
- Well-established audiology laboratory
- Paediatric focus
- Limited capacity compared to need

---

## PUBLIC AWARENESS GAP

### Knowledge Deficit
- **General public unaware** of available rehabilitation services
- Don't know where to seek help for hearing problems
- Limited understanding of audiology vs ENT vs hearing aid dealers
- Stigma around hearing loss and disability

### Professional Recognition
- Audiology profession not well understood
- Often confused with "hearing aid sales"
- Limited awareness of scope of practice
- Need for public education campaigns

---

## DEMAND-SUPPLY MISMATCH

### The Gap
- **Demand for services EXCEEDS supply** dramatically
- Acute shortage affects entire continuum:
  - Screening → Diagnosis → Treatment → Rehabilitation → Follow-up
- Wait times for government services prolonged
- Private services unaffordable for majority

### Population Reach Calculation
**Current State**:
- ~40 audiology technicians in government sector
- ~36 private audiology centres (unknown staff numbers)
- Estimated **<100 practicing audiologists nationwide**

**Coverage Gap**:
- 1.9 million people with hearing loss
- If 100 audiologists: **19,000 patients per audiologist**
- Impossible to provide adequate care

---

## URBAN-RURAL DIVIDE (CRITICAL FOR AI DISCUSSION)

### Urban Reality (Colombo/Western Province)
- Multiple private audiology centres
- Tertiary care hospitals with ENT departments
- Access to cochlear implants, advanced diagnostics
- Hearing aid availability (though cost barrier remains)
- Vision Care: 15 specialized branches islandwide (but concentrated urban)

### Rural Reality
- **No audiology services** in most districts
- Patients must travel to Colombo (cost, time, burden)
- Delayed diagnosis and intervention
- School children unscreened
- Occupational hearing loss undiagnosed
- Elderly population unserved

### Travel Barrier
- Rural patient to Colombo: 4-8 hours travel, accommodation costs
- For multiple audiology visits (initial assessment, hearing aid fitting, follow-up)
- Economic burden prevents care-seeking
- **THIS IS WHERE TELEMEDICINE + AI COULD TRANSFORM ACCESS**

## ECONOMIC CONTEXT

### Healthcare System
- **Dual system**: Public (free) + Private (out-of-pocket)
- Public sector: Limited audiology services, long waits
- Private sector: Better services, unaffordable for many
- **No national hearing screening programme** (unlike vision)
- Hearing aids **not covered** by government insurance

### Affordability Challenge
- Hearing aids: Rs. 50,000 - 300,000 (~USD 150-900)
- Cochlear implants: Rs. 2-4 million (~USD 6,000-12,000)
- Monthly median income: ~Rs. 40,000 (~USD 120)
- **Hearing aids = 1-7 months salary**

### Private Sector Providers
- Vision Care (15 branches)
- Gift of Sound (Ratmalana Audiology Centre)
- Hearcare
- Wickramarachchi Hearing Care
- Multiple Colombo-based centres

## EDUCATION & SPECIAL NEEDS

### Deaf Education
- Separate schools for deaf/HoH students
- Limited integration in mainstream schools
- **Severe shortage of sign language interpreters**
- Educational outcomes below hearing peers
- Limited vocational opportunities

### School Screening
- Not universal or systematic
- Gampaha study showed 5.5% prevalence
- Many children reach school age undiagnosed
- Late intervention = poor language outcomes

## KEY OPPORTUNITIES FOR AI INTEGRATION

### 1. GEOGRAPHIC EXTENSION
**Problem**: 95% hospitals lack audiology services, 10 districts with NO services
**AI Solution**:
- AI otoscopy in primary care clinics nationwide
- Telemedicine audiology with AI interpretation
- Mobile screening units with AI diagnostic support

### 2. WORKFORCE AMPLIFICATION
**Problem**: 0.18 per 100,000 (need 1-2 per 100,000) - 10x shortage
**AI Solution**:
- AI handles routine screening/diagnosis
- Audiologists focus on complex cases, rehabilitation, counseling
- One audiologist + AI can serve 10x more patients

### 3. SKILL DEMOCRATIZATION
**Problem**: Audiology technicians vs audiologists, no specialists in rural areas
**AI Solution**:
- AI provides "specialist-level" diagnostic support
- Standardizes quality across different skill levels
- Training tool for technicians

### 4. EARLY DETECTION
**Problem**: No systematic screening, late diagnosis common
**AI Solution**:
- School-based AI otoscopy screening
- Occupational health AI audiometry
- Well-baby clinic automated OAE + AI interpretation

### 5. COST REDUCTION
**Problem**: Multiple Colombo visits for assessment/fitting = major economic burden
**AI Solution**:
- Fewer visits through telemedicine + AI
- Reduced travel costs
- Faster diagnosis-to-treatment pathway
- AI optimization of hearing aid fitting (fewer adjustment visits)

## BARRIERS TO AI IMPLEMENTATION

### 1. Infrastructure
- Internet connectivity in rural areas (improving but patchy)
- Electricity reliability
- Hardware costs (computers, tablets, digital equipment)

### 2. Training
- Current workforce not trained in AI tool use
- Need curriculum integration at University of Kelaniya
- Continuing education for existing professionals

### 3. Regulatory
- No clear framework for medical AI approval in Sri Lanka
- Data privacy concerns
- Liability questions (who is responsible if AI makes error?)

### 4. Cultural
- Professional resistance ("AI will replace us")
- Patient trust in technology
- Preference for face-to-face consultation

### 5. Economic
- Initial investment costs
- Subscription models vs one-time purchase
- Who pays? Government budget constraints

## COMPARATIVE CONTEXT

### Regional Comparison
- Sri Lanka situation **similar to other South Asian countries**
- Better than some (Bangladesh, Pakistan) in professional training
- Worse than some (India has larger audiology workforce, more training centres)
- Similar workforce crisis seen even in developed countries (UK, US)

### What Sri Lanka Has Going For It
1. **Recognized professional cadre** (legal framework exists)
2. **University training programme** (Kelaniya) - can integrate AI education
3. **Existing private sector** - innovation testing ground
4. **Government healthcare infrastructure** (647 hospitals - physical reach exists)
5. **Digital literacy increasing** - smartphone penetration growing
6. **National health data system** - Ministry of Health data portal
